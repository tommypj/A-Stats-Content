## Built-In Keyword Research

A-Stats includes a keyword research tool directly inside the platform so you do not need a separate subscription to a tool like Ahrefs or Semrush for basic keyword discovery. You can find target keywords, evaluate their difficulty, and feed them straight into your content outlines — all without leaving the editor.

The keyword research tool is accessible from two places: the main **Keywords** section in the left navigation, and the keyword field inside any article or outline editor (click "Research Keywords" next to the field).

---

## Finding Keywords

### Starting a Search

Enter a seed keyword — a broad topic or phrase related to your content — into the search bar at the top of the Keywords page. Press Enter or click Search.

The tool returns a list of related keyword suggestions ranked by estimated monthly search volume. Each result shows:

- **Keyword phrase** — the full search query
- **Monthly search volume** — estimated searches per month in your target market
- **Keyword difficulty (KD)** — a 0–100 score indicating how hard it is to rank on page one
- **Intent signal** — informational, commercial, navigational, or transactional

> Tip: Start with a broad 2–3 word seed keyword. The tool surfaces long-tail variations that are often easier to rank for than the head term.

### Filtering Results

Use the filters above the results table to narrow the list:

- **Volume range** — set a minimum and maximum monthly search volume
- **Difficulty range** — filter to only show keywords below a certain difficulty threshold
- **Keyword contains** — filter to keywords that include a specific word or phrase
- **Exclude keywords** — hide results containing words you do not want (e.g., competitor brand names)

## Understanding Search Volume and Difficulty

### Search Volume

Search volume is an estimate of how many times a keyword is searched per month in a given country. Higher volume means more potential traffic if you rank for the term.

Volume thresholds to keep in mind:

| Volume | Context |
|---|---|
| 100,000+ | Head terms — very competitive, slow to rank |
| 10,000–100,000 | Mid-tail — competitive but achievable for established sites |
| 1,000–10,000 | Long-tail — good balance of traffic and attainability |
| 100–1,000 | Niche long-tail — lower traffic but often high conversion |
| Below 100 | Very niche — worth targeting in bulk for programmatic SEO |

> Tip: A keyword with 500 monthly searches and a difficulty of 20 will typically drive more real traffic to a new site than one with 50,000 searches and a difficulty of 85.

### Keyword Difficulty

Keyword difficulty (KD) is scored 0–100. It is based on the strength of the pages currently ranking on page one. A higher score means the top results are from high-authority domains with many backlinks — harder to displace.

| KD Score | What It Means |
|---|---|
| 0–20 | Low — new sites can rank with good content |
| 21–40 | Moderate — achievable with some domain authority |
| 41–60 | Competitive — requires backlinks and strong content |
| 61–80 | Hard — established authority needed |
| 81–100 | Very hard — typically dominated by major publishers |

> Tip: New content sites should target keywords with a KD below 30 to build early rankings and domain authority before going after harder terms.

## Choosing a Target Keyword

The best target keyword for a piece of content is one that balances three things:

1. **Relevance** — it matches what the article is actually about
2. **Attainability** — the difficulty is within range of your current domain authority
3. **Intent alignment** — the search intent matches what you are offering (information, a product, a service)

### Evaluating Intent

Intent signals tell you what the searcher expects to find:

- **Informational** — "how to", "what is", "guide to" — the reader wants to learn something. Best for blog articles.
- **Commercial** — "best", "vs", "review" — the reader is comparing options before buying. Good for comparison articles.
- **Transactional** — "buy", "price", "discount" — the reader wants to make a purchase. Best for product or landing pages.
- **Navigational** — a brand name or specific site — the reader is looking for a particular page. Usually not worth targeting unless it is your own brand.

> Tip: Targeting a keyword whose intent does not match your content type is a common mistake. A product page cannot rank for an informational query, and vice versa.

### Checking the SERP

Before committing to a keyword, click the keyword row to open the SERP preview. This shows you the types of pages currently ranking (articles, product pages, forums, videos) and gives you a clearer picture of what Google expects to see for that query.

## Using Keywords in Outlines

Once you have chosen a target keyword, you can send it directly to the outline generator:

1. Check the checkbox next to the keyword in the research results.
2. Click **Create Outline** in the toolbar that appears at the bottom of the page.
3. The outline generator opens with the keyword pre-filled. Click **Generate Outline** to produce an AI-generated structure built around that keyword.

You can also copy a keyword to your clipboard and paste it into the keyword field of any existing article or outline.

### Secondary Keywords

The keyword research tool also surfaces **secondary keywords** — related terms that frequently appear alongside the primary keyword in top-ranking articles. These are listed below the main result when you expand a keyword row.

Adding secondary keywords to your outline helps the AI generator produce articles that cover the topic comprehensively, which is a signal Google uses to evaluate topical authority.

> Tip: Do not try to rank a single article for dozens of keywords. Choose one primary keyword and 3–5 closely related secondary keywords. Separate articles for separate topics.

## Saved Keywords and Lists

You can save keywords to named lists for future reference:

1. Click the bookmark icon on any keyword row.
2. Choose an existing list or create a new one.
3. Access saved lists from **Keywords > Saved Lists** in the left navigation.

Saved keyword lists are per-project, so your research stays organized by the site or client you are working on.
